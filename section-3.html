Fixed Income Fundamentals

Learning Objective: Understand the fixed income business, including debt products, bonds, and money markets. Learn how bonds work and why investors use them.

What is Fixed Income?

Fixed income refers to investment securities that pay a fixed rate of return. The primary types of fixed income products are bonds and money market instruments. These products are essential to capital markets and provide investors with regular income streams.

Debt Products: Understanding the Basics

How Traditional Loans Work:

  • Lender loans capital to borrower
  • Borrower repays capital plus interest over agreed period
  • Simple bilateral relationship between lender and borrower

How Bonds Work (Alternative to Loans):

  • Issuer (government or company) raises capital by issuing bonds
  • Investors purchase bonds at par value (issue price × quantity)
  • Issuer repays investors' capital at maturity plus periodic coupon payments
  • Allows capital raising from many investors instead of single lender
  • Bonds can be traded, unlike traditional loans

Key Advantage: Bonds allow borrowers to raise large amounts of capital from many investors simultaneously, and investors can buy and sell bonds in secondary markets.

Coupons: Regular Interest Payments

Definition: Coupons are the regular interest payments made by the bond issuer to bondholders.

Payment Frequency:

  • Traditionally: Semi-annual coupon payments (twice per year)
  • Also: Annual payments
  • Increasingly: Electronic trading eliminates need for physical coupons

Example: A bond with a 5% coupon rate and $1,000 par value pays $50 per year (typically $25 every 6 months).

Short-Term Debt Instruments

Commercial Paper (CP):

  • Maturity: Usually 1-6 months
  • Issued by: Banks and large corporations
  • Type: Unsecured debt (no collateral)
  • Advantages: Faster approval than business loans

US Treasury Bills (T-Bills):

  • Issuer: US Government
  • Maturities: Up to just less than 1 year
  • Denominations: $1,000 minimum
  • Pricing: Issued at a discount from par (face value)
  • Repayment: Paid at par on maturity (buyer keeps the discount as interest)
  • Safety: Backed by US government (lowest risk)

SEC Registration: Short-term debt under 270 days doesn't require SEC registration, reducing issuance costs.

Repos: Repurchase Agreements

What is a Repo? A "repo" (repurchase agreement) is a short-term loan where the borrower sells securities with an agreement to repurchase them at a higher price the next day.

Typical Use: Investment banks use repos to meet fractional reserve requirements and manage short-term liquidity needs.

How a Repo Works:

  • Day T: Repo seller provides assets to lender and receives cash
  • Overnight: Lender holds securities as collateral protection
  • Day T+1: Repo seller repurchases assets with fee/interest

Example: An investment bank needs $1 million to meet reserve requirements. They repo (sell with buy-back agreement) $1 million in Treasury securities to a lender overnight. The next day, they repurchase the Treasuries plus pay a small fee.

Reverse Repo: The opposite transaction where a dealer borrows assets and lends money.

Repo Rates: Usually lower than money market rates because the lender is protected by collateral (government securities), making the transaction lower risk.

Why Invest in Bonds?

Investors are attracted to fixed income for several reasons:

  • Regular Income: Predictable coupon payments provide steady cash flow
  • Capital Preservation: Bonds return principal at maturity
  • Lower Volatility: Less risky than equities
  • Portfolio Diversification: Different risk/return profile than stocks
  • Predictable Returns: Known interest payments and maturity date
  • Safety (Government Bonds): Backed by government credit

Bonds and Interest Rates

Understanding the relationship between bond prices and interest rates is critical to fixed income investing and trading.

The Inverse Relationship: Bonds and Interest Rates

Fundamental Principle: Bond prices and interest rates have an inverse relationship—when one goes up, the other goes down.

Why This Happens:

  • Higher Interest Rates: New bonds offer higher coupon payments, making existing bonds with lower coupons less attractive. Their prices fall to compete.
  • Lower Interest Rates: New bonds offer lower coupon payments, making existing bonds with higher coupons more attractive. Their prices rise.

Visual Relationship:

  • Higher interest rates → Lower bond prices
  • Lower interest rates → Higher bond prices

Price Convergence to Par: As a bond approaches its maturity date, its price trends toward par value (face value), regardless of current interest rates. On the maturity date, the bondholder receives exactly par value.

The Coupon Rate vs. Interest Rate Spread

Key Principle: The bigger the difference between a bond's coupon rate and the current interest rate, the bigger the price change when rates change.

Example 1: Small Spread

  • Bond coupon rate: 4%
  • Current interest rate: 3.8%
  • Spread: 0.2% (small difference)
  • Price will be only slightly above par

Example 2: Large Spread

  • Bond coupon rate: 5%
  • Current interest rate: 3% (rates have fallen)
  • Spread: 2% (large difference)
  • Price will be significantly above par

Duration: Measuring Interest Rate Risk

Definition: Duration is a measure of a bond's sensitivity to interest rate changes. It measures how long it takes, in years, for an investor to be repaid through the bond's total cash flows.

Important Note: Duration is NOT the same as maturity. Duration accounts for all the coupon payments received before maturity, while maturity is just the final repayment date.

Duration Calculation: Duration is expressed in years and represents how much the bond price will change for each 1% change in interest rates.

Duration Example:

  • Bond A: Duration of 5 years
  • Interest rate increase of 1%
  • Price change: Approximately -5% (price drops 5%)

Another Example:

  • Bond B: Duration of 2 years
  • Interest rate decrease of 1%
  • Price change: Approximately +2% (price rises 2%)

Key Insight: Higher duration bonds experience bigger price swings when interest rates change. This is critical if you plan to sell a bond before maturity—your return depends on the selling price, not just the coupon.

Yield on a Bond

Definition: Yield is the total return an investor receives from a bond, measured as the interest on their investment.

Relationship to Price: As bond price rises, yield falls. As bond price falls, yield rises.

Why This Happens:

  • Coupon payment remains fixed
  • When price rises, the fixed coupon represents a smaller return on investment
  • When price falls, the fixed coupon represents a larger return on investment

Example 1: Price Rise (Safety Premium)

  • You invest £100 in a 5% bond at par value
  • Your yield = 5/100 = 5%
  • Bond is considered safe (high quality)
  • Bond price rises to £120 (investors want it for safety)
  • Your yield now = 5/120 = 4.2% (yield falls)

Example 2: Price Fall (Risk Premium)

  • You invest £100 in a 6% bond at par value
  • Your yield = 6/100 = 6%
  • Bond is considered risky (company troubles)
  • Bond price falls to £85 (investors demand compensation for risk)
  • Your yield now = 6/85 = 7% (yield rises to compensate for risk)

Junk Bonds: Bonds with below-par prices (indicating high risk) are known as "high yield" bonds. These offer higher yields but carry substantial default risk.

Formula: Yield = Annual Coupon Payment / Current Bond Price

Bond Types and Credit Ratings

The fixed income market includes many different types of bonds with varying risk and return characteristics.

Bond Categories

Fixed income products can be categorized in multiple ways:

By Issuer Type:

  • Government Bonds: Issued by national governments
  • Corporate Bonds: Issued by companies
  • Municipal Bonds: Issued by state and local governments
  • Sovereign Debt: Government bonds issued internationally

By Maturity:

  • Short-term: Less than 1-5 years
  • Intermediate: 5-10 years
  • Long-term: 10-50+ years

By Interest Type:

  • Fixed Rate: Coupon rate stays the same throughout bond life
  • Floating Rate: Coupon rate adjusts based on market interest rates
  • Zero Coupon: No coupon payments; issued at deep discount

Bond Grades and Credit Ratings

Purpose: Credit ratings indicate the issuer's ability to repay the bond and the risk of default.

Rating Categories:

Investment Grade Bonds (Lower Risk):

  • AAA: Highest quality. Government bonds or very high-quality corporates. Lowest default risk
  • AA: Very high quality. Strong financial position
  • A: High quality. Favorable financial position
  • BBB: Upper medium grade. Adequate financial capacity to meet obligations

High Yield / Junk Bonds (Higher Risk):

  • BB: Non-investment grade. Speculative. Some uncertainty
  • B: Speculative. Minimal financial stability
  • CCC: Substantial risks. Poor financial position
  • CC: Extremely speculative. Very high default risk

Key Principle: Higher credit ratings = lower risk = lower yields. Lower credit ratings = higher risk = higher yields to compensate.

Municipal Bonds

Definition: Bonds issued by states, cities, counties, and other governmental entities (not the national government).

Use of Proceeds:

  • Finance day-to-day government operations
  • Fund major infrastructure projects (schools, highways, bridges)
  • Build public facilities

Tax Advantage: Interest income from municipal bonds is typically exempt from federal income tax (and sometimes state taxes). This allows municipalities to issue bonds at lower rates than corporations.

Example Municipal Bond:

  • Issuer: Huntington Beach California Union High School District
  • Purpose: 2016 Refunding and School Financing Projects
  • Coupon: 2%
  • Maturity: September 1, 2027
  • Rating: AA (very high quality)
  • Initial Price: $97.825
  • Note: Lower coupon (2%) due to tax advantage

Real-World Bond Examples

Example 1: Short-term Corporate Bond

  • Issuer: Marks and Spencer (retailer)
  • Term: 5-year fixed corporate rate bond
  • Coupon: 6.125%
  • Maturity: July 10, 2024
  • Price: £87.6 per £100 par (trading below par—indicates higher risk)
  • Issue Size: £400 million
  • Minimum Denomination: £1,000
  • Analysis: Below-par price suggests company is considered riskier or interest rates have risen since issuance

Example 2: Long-term Corporate Bond

  • Issuer: Tesco (retailer)
  • Term: 50-year fixed corporate rate bond (very long maturity)
  • Coupon: 5.2%
  • Maturity: March 5, 2057
  • Price: £90.75 per £100 par (trading below par)
  • Issue Size: £287.5 million
  • Minimum Denomination: £50,000 (larger denomination = institutional bond)
  • Analysis: 50-year bond is very long—duration will be high, making price very sensitive to interest rate changes

Bond Markets and Trading

The bond market is one of the largest financial markets in the world. Understanding how bonds are issued and traded is essential to fixed income trading.

Primary Bond Market: New Issuances

Process: When a bond issuer wants to raise capital by selling new bonds to investors.

Steps in Primary Market:

  1. Issuer Engagement: Prospective issuer (government or company) engages an investment bank
  2. Underwriting: The investment bank may underwrite the bonds (agree to buy them if they don't sell)
  3. Offering: Bank offers bonds for resale to brokers or institutional clients
  4. Distribution: Bonds are distributed to investors at the offering price
  5. Profit: Investment banks earn profit on the difference between their purchase price and the public offering price

Investment Bank Role: Investment banks act as intermediaries, helping issuers sell their bonds and coordinating with investors. They take on the risk that bonds may not sell at anticipated prices.

Secondary Bond Market: Trading Existing Bonds

What is the Secondary Market? The market where existing bonds (previously issued) are bought and sold among investors.

Key Characteristics:

  • Bonds can be resold multiple times after initial issuance
  • Price varies based on interest rates, credit quality, and market conditions
  • Provides liquidity—investors can convert bonds to cash
  • Larger volume and liquidity than primary market

Over-the-Counter (OTC) Bond Trading

Definition: Most bonds trade OTC (over-the-counter)—not on centralized exchanges like stock exchanges.

Market Participants:

  • Brokers: Facilitate trades between buyers and sellers
  • Investment Banks: Trade their own inventory and facilitate client trades
  • Institutional Investors: Pension funds, mutual funds, insurance companies

Advantages of OTC:

  • Customizable terms and sizes
  • Can trade bonds not available on exchanges
  • Direct negotiation between parties

Disadvantages:

  • Less transparent than exchange trading
  • Higher spreads (bid-ask spreads wider than stocks)
  • Less price discovery

Electronic Bond Trading Platforms

Growing Trend: Small volume corporate bonds increasingly trade on electronic platforms.

Major Electronic Platforms:

  • LiquidNet: Institutional bond trading
  • MarketAxess: Electronic bond trading (now major platform)
  • TradeWeb: Fixed income and derivatives trading
  • Bloomberg: Bloomberg terminals include bond trading functionality

Benefits of Electronic Trading:

  • Better price transparency
  • Faster execution
  • Reduced spreads
  • More efficient matching of buyers and sellers

Bond Market Structure

Size and Importance: The global bond market is significantly larger than the equity market, with trillions of dollars in outstanding debt.

Key Participants:

  • Issuers: Governments, corporations, municipal authorities
  • Investors: Institutional funds, individuals, central banks
  • Intermediaries: Banks, brokers, dealers
  • Regulators: Central banks, securities regulators

Market Functions:

  • Price discovery—markets determine fair value
  • Capital allocation—direct capital from savers to borrowers
  • Liquidity provision—investors can buy/sell easily
  • Risk transfer—spread credit risk among many investors

Advanced Bond Concepts and Applications

Beyond basic bond mechanics, fixed income professionals use advanced concepts for valuation, trading, and risk management.

Understanding Bond Math Through Examples

Example Bond: Marks and Spencer Corporate Bond

  • Coupon: 6.125%
  • Maturity: July 10, 2024 (5-year from issuance)
  • Price: £87.6 per £100 par
  • Denomination: £1,000

Calculation Questions:

Question 1: What is the total par value if buying minimum amount?

  • Minimum denomination: £1,000 par value
  • Answer: £1,000

Question 2: What is the total coupon value?

  • Annual coupon: 6.125% × £1,000 = £61.25
  • For 5-year bond: £61.25 × 5 = £306.25
  • Answer: £306.25

Question 3: How much at maturity?

  • Bondholder receives par value: £1,000
  • Plus final coupon (if maturity hasn't passed): £30.625 (6-month coupon)
  • Answer: £1,000

Question 4: What is the yield?

  • Purchase price: £87.6 per £100 = £876 for £1,000 bond
  • Yield = Coupon / Price = £61.25 / £876 = 7%
  • Answer: 7% (higher than coupon due to discount purchase)

Market Sensitivity: Interest Rate Changes

Scenario: Greek Bailout Bond Exercise

Question: Why does optimism about a Greek bailout deal cause bond yields to fall?

Answer Explanation:

  • Optimism: Market becomes more confident Greek government will repay debt
  • Risk Perception Falls: Default risk decreases
  • Bond Demand Rises: Investors become willing to buy Greek bonds
  • Prices Rise: Increased demand pushes bond prices higher
  • Yields Fall: Higher bond prices mean lower yields
  • Relationship: Price ↑ → Yield ↓ (inverse relationship)

Economic Impact: Lower yields reduce the government's borrowing costs, making refinancing debt easier and cheaper.

Duration and Interest Rate Sensitivity

Practical Application: Understanding duration helps traders and investors:

  • Predict price changes when interest rates move
  • Manage interest rate risk
  • Compare bonds with different maturities
  • Time bond purchases (buy when rates about to fall)

Long-term Bond Example: Tesco 50-year Bond

  • Maturity: 2057 (50 years into future)
  • Duration: Approximately 15-20 years (very high)
  • Interest Rate Sensitivity: Very high—price will fluctuate significantly with rate changes
  • Trading Implication: Small interest rate moves = large profit/loss opportunities
  • Investment Strategy: Only for investors with strong conviction on interest rate direction

Yield Curves and Market Outlook

What is a Yield Curve? A graph showing bond yields at different maturities (short-term, intermediate, long-term).

Normal Yield Curve:

  • Long-term bonds have higher yields than short-term bonds
  • Indicates normal economic conditions
  • Investors demand higher yields for tying up money longer

Inverted Yield Curve:

  • Short-term bonds have higher yields than long-term bonds (unusual)
  • Often signals economic recession ahead
  • Investors want to lock in long-term rates before they fall

Flat Yield Curve:

  • Yields are similar across all maturities
  • Indicates transition in market outlook

Bond Ladder Strategy

Concept: Rather than buying all bonds of one maturity, buy bonds maturing at regular intervals.

Benefits:

  • Reduces interest rate risk (not all at one maturity)
  • Provides regular cash flow as bonds mature
  • Allows reinvestment at different rates
  • Simpler than active trading

Example: Instead of buying $10,000 of 10-year bonds, buy:

  • $2,000 of 2-year bonds
  • $2,000 of 4-year bonds
  • $2,000 of 6-year bonds
  • $2,000 of 8-year bonds
  • $2,000 of 10-year bonds

Fixed Income Trading Strategies

Carry Trade: Buy higher-yielding bonds and earn the coupon payment while waiting for price appreciation.

Value Play: Buy bonds trading below intrinsic value, expecting price recovery.

Curve Positioning: Take positions based on expected yield curve movements.

Credit Positioning: Buy higher-yield corporate bonds when credit spreads are attractive.

Hedging: Use government bonds to hedge credit risk in corporate bond portfolios.

Key Takeaways for Fixed Income Professionals

  • Understand duration to manage interest rate risk
  • Monitor credit ratings and spreads for value opportunities
  • Know the relationship between bonds and interest rates
  • Understand market structure (primary vs. secondary, OTC vs. electronic)
  • Use mathematical tools to evaluate bond value
  • Consider multiple factors: yield, duration, credit quality, maturity
  • Keep current on economic indicators that affect bond prices
