Currency and Foreign Exchange Markets

Learning Objective: Understand the foreign exchange (FX) business, how currencies are traded, and the mechanisms for managing foreign exchange risk.

What is Foreign Exchange (FX)?

Definition: The marketplace in which various national currencies are traded.

Scale: The forex market is the largest, most liquid market in the world, with trillions of dollars traded per day.

Trading Hours: 24-hour trading available Sunday–Friday (GMT). However, there are three main sessions across Asia, Europe, and the US.

Session Characteristics:

  • Although sessions overlap, main currencies trade primarily in their regional sessions
  • Example: USD trading does most volume during US trading session
  • Tokyo session (Asia Pacific): Trades Asian currencies, USD
  • London session (Europe): Trades EUR, GBP, trades highest volume
  • New York session (US): Trades USD, highest volatility

Market Structure: No centralized location. It is an electronic network of banks, brokers, institutions, and individual traders.

Trading Instruments: Forwards and futures can be used to trade in the FX market, in addition to spot transactions.

How FX Trading Works

Spot Market: The marketplace where financial instruments such as commodities, currencies, and securities are traded for immediate delivery.

Settlement Terms:

  • Standard: FX spot trades settle on a T+2 basis (two business days after trade)
  • Exception: USD/CAD settles in 1 business day

Trading Lots: Currency is traded in various sized lots

  • Micro lot: 1,000 units of your currency
  • Mini lot: 10,000 units of your base currency
  • Standard lot: 100,000 units of your base currency

Base Currency: The currency your account is funded in. This is the reference currency for all transactions.

Currency Pairs

How Trading Works: Currency trading is done in pairs. You buy one currency and simultaneously sell another.

Example:

  • Trade EUR/USD: Buy euros, sell US dollars
  • Bet that euro will strengthen against dollar
  • Profit if euro appreciates relative to dollar

Market Concentration: Majority of volume in currency trading is confined to only 18 currency pairs.

Eight Most Traded Currencies:

  • USD (US Dollar) – Most traded globally
  • EUR (Euro) – Second most traded
  • GBP (British Pound) – Major currency
  • JPY (Japanese Yen) – Major currency
  • CHF (Swiss Franc) – Safe haven currency
  • CAD (Canadian Dollar)
  • AUD (Australian Dollar)
  • NZD (New Zealand Dollar)

Major Currency Pairs:

  • EUR/USD – Most traded pair
  • USD/JPY – Second most traded
  • GBP/USD
  • USD/CHF
  • AUD/USD
  • USD/CAD

Challenges in FX Trading

Complexity: With equity trading, you are making a decision based on one company's value. With FX, you are valuing an entire country!

Long-Term Analysis: Market analysis to predict long-term trends is complex. Requires understanding of:

  • National economic policies
  • Interest rate differentials
  • Trade balances
  • Political stability
  • Inflation rates

Short-Term Trading: Some traders focus strictly on short-term trading based on news releases.

Key Economic Indicators That Move Markets: Traders monitor these closely for immediate market moves:

  • 1. Non-Farm Payrolls (NFP): US employment data—most volatile indicator
  • 2. Purchasing Managers Index (PMI): Manufacturing activity
  • 3. Consumer Price Index (CPI): Inflation measure
  • 4. Retail Sales: Consumer spending patterns
  • 5. Durable Goods Orders: Long-term consumer spending
  • 6. Federal Reserve Announcements: Even Fed Chairman's comments on interest rates can trigger market moves

Volatility Sources: Currency markets react quickly to unexpected news, making FX trading risky but also offering opportunities.

FX Trading Venues

Electronic Network: FX trading is conducted across an electronic network rather than having a physical location.

Major Trading Platforms and Brokers:

  • HotSpot: Electronic trading platform for foreign exchange
  • CurrenEx: Electronic currency trading system
  • FXall: Bloomberg's FX trading platform
  • BrokerTec: Fixed income and FX trading platform

Major Banks and Dealers:

  • Deutsche Bank
  • Morgan Stanley
  • Bank of America Merrill Lynch (BAML)
  • Other major investment banks and FX specialists

Market Structure: These platforms and institutions act as market makers, providing liquidity and narrower bid-ask spreads for traders.

Interest Rate Parity (IRP) and Forward Rates

Concept: Interest Rate Parity explains the relationship between spot exchange rates, forward exchange rates, and interest rates in two countries.

Principle: Forward exchange rate should adjust for interest rate differences between two countries, so arbitrage opportunities do not exist.

Example Scenario:

  • Starting Position: You have $1,000,000 USD and want to invest for 90 days
  • Spot Rate: 1 USD = 1.4800 Swiss Francs (SF)
  • USD Interest Rate: 8% per annum (2% for 90 days)
  • CHF Interest Rate: 4% per annum (1% for 90 days)

Two Strategies:

Strategy 1: Invest in USD

  • Invest $1,000,000 at 2% for 90 days
  • Receive: $1,020,000

Strategy 2: Convert to CHF, Invest, Convert Back

  • Convert $1,000,000 to CHF at spot (1.4800): SF 1,480,000
  • Invest SF 1,480,000 at 1% for 90 days: SF 1,494,800
  • Convert back at forward rate (1.4655): $1,019,993

Result: Both strategies yield approximately equal returns ($1,020,000 vs $1,019,993), demonstrating interest rate parity.

Forward Rate Formula: The forward rate adjusts to eliminate arbitrage opportunities between investing in each currency.

FX Swaps

Definition: A transaction in which two parties exchange principal amounts in different currencies at the spot rate, then exchange them back at a forward rate.

How an FX Swap Works:

Upfront (Day 0):

  • Party A needs $1 million
  • Party B has £800,000
  • Exchange at spot rate: GBP/USD = 1.25
  • Party A gives £800,000, receives $1 million

At Contract Maturity (e.g., 90 days later):

  • Exchange back at forward rate: GBP/USD = 2.0
  • Party A returns $1 million, receives £500,000
  • Or: equivalent value exchanged based on forward rate

Purpose: Allows parties to borrow in needed currency while managing exchange rate risk.

No Interest Payments: FX swaps typically involve no interest payments—only principal exchange at spot and forward rates.

Currency Swaps (Cross-Currency Swaps)

Definition: A contract to exchange principal amounts in different currencies at the outset, exchange interest payments during the term, then re-exchange principal at maturity.

Key Difference from FX Swaps: Currency swaps include interest payments, while FX swaps typically do not.

How a Currency Swap Works:

Upfront (Day 0):

  • Exchange principal: $1 million ↔ £800,000
  • Exchange occurs at spot rate (GBP/USD = 1.25)

During Contract Term (Regular Payments):

  • Party A pays interest in GBP (in pounds)
  • Party B pays interest in USD (in dollars)
  • Interest rates typically fixed or floating, based on agreement
  • Payments linked to interest rates in each currency

At Contract Maturity:

  • Re-exchange principal amounts (at original spot rate or agreed forward rate)
  • Each party receives back their original currency

Purpose: Allows companies to borrow in foreign currency at more favorable rates than if borrowing directly from local market.

Risk Management: Protects against both exchange rate risk AND interest rate risk.

FX Swaps vs. Currency Swaps

FX Swap Characteristics:

  • No exchange of interest during contract term
  • Differing amounts of funds exchanged at end (different at forward rate than at spot)
  • Typically used to offset exchange rate risk only
  • Simpler structure, shorter duration

Currency Swap Characteristics:

  • Interest payments exchanged throughout contract term
  • Principal and interest payments exchanged
  • Typically used to offset both exchange rate AND interest rate risk
  • More complex, longer duration (often multi-year)

When to Use Each:

  • FX Swaps: Short-term funding needs in foreign currency
  • Currency Swaps: Long-term borrowing in foreign currency with interest rate exposure

Exchange Rate Pass-Through

Definition: The degree to which changes in exchange rates are reflected in the prices of imported and exported goods.

Concept: When a currency appreciates or depreciates, import and export prices change, but not necessarily proportionally.

Example Scenario: BMW Automobile

Initial Situation:

  • BMW made in Germany
  • Price in euros: €28,000
  • Spot rate: EUR/USD = 1.25
  • US price: $35,000 (€28,000 × 1.25)

Currency Movement:

  • Euro appreciates by 20%
  • New spot rate: EUR/USD = 1.50
  • Without pass-through adjustment: €28,000 × 1.50 = $42,000

What Happens in Practice:

  • BMW doesn't want to price themselves out of US market
  • BMW accepts lower euro profits
  • BMW reduces euro price or accepts margin compression
  • US price increases to only $40,000 (not $42,000)

Pass-Through Calculation:

  • Price increase: $40,000 - $35,000 = $5,000
  • Exchange rate change: 20%
  • Degree of pass-through: $5,000 / $7,000 = 71%
  • Only 71% of exchange rate change passed through to prices

Why Not 100% Pass-Through?

  • Companies want to maintain market share
  • Price elasticity of demand—higher prices reduce sales volume
  • Competition from other manufacturers
  • Overseas production to hedge currency moves

Economic Implications:

  • Partial pass-through reduces export competitiveness after currency appreciation
  • Imports become more expensive but not as much as exchange rate change suggests
  • Trade balances affected differently than pure exchange rate changes would indicate

Key Takeaways for FX Trading

  • FX is the world's largest financial market with 24-hour trading
  • Trading happens in currency pairs on electronic networks
  • Economic data releases drive short-term volatility
  • Interest rate differentials affect forward rates (IRP)
  • FX Swaps manage short-term currency exposure
  • Currency Swaps manage long-term currency and interest rate exposure
  • Exchange rate pass-through is incomplete—companies manage pricing strategically
  • FX trading requires understanding macro factors: interest rates, inflation, trade, politics
