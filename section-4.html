Derivatives: Foundations and Applications

Learning Objective: Understand the derivatives trading business, including the main products, their uses, and their applications in hedging and speculation.

What Are Derivatives?

Definition: A derivative is a financial contract whose value is based on the value of underlying assets.

Historical Context: The concept of derivatives is not new. Derivatives have been around for generations, particularly in the farming industry. For example, a farmer might contract to sell wheat on a particular date at a particular price, protecting against price drops.

Modern Uses: Today, derivatives have numerous applications, mostly used for:

  • Hedging (reducing risk)
  • Risk management
  • Speculation (betting on price movements)

Underlying Assets: Derivatives can be written on virtually any underlying asset:

  • Commodities (wheat, oil, metals)
  • Bonds
  • Interest Rates
  • Foreign Exchange (FX)
  • Credit instruments
  • Mortgages and Asset-Backed Securities
  • Equities

Key Derivatives Terms

  • Counterparty: The party taking the other side of the trade
  • Strike Price: The price at which a derivative (option) can be exercised
  • Expiry: The date at which the derivative contract expires
  • Hedging: Insuring yourself against a negative event. Hedging doesn't stop the event from happening, but it reduces its impact

Types of Derivatives

1. Forwards

  • Agree to buy or sell something at a specific point in time in the future for a specific price
  • Privately negotiated (OTC)
  • Terms are fully negotiable between parties
  • Price established at inception of contract
  • Higher risk than futures—both sides exposed to credit risk (no clearing house)
  • Mainly used for hedging

Example: "I'll purchase 4 tons of wheat from you at $2,000 a ton in six months." Six months later: the buyer gets 4 tons of wheat and pays $8,000.

2. Futures

  • A forward contract traded on an exchange
  • Highly standardized contracts
  • Standard sizes and maturity dates
  • Profits/losses settled each day (daily marking-to-market)
  • Traded on exchanges like CME, Euronext, LME, ICE Futures Europe, Eurex
  • Central counterparty clearing (exchanges act as clearinghouse)
  • Lower risk than forwards due to daily settlement

Example: Chicago SRW Wheat Futures Contract specifications:

  • Contract Unit: 5,000 bushels (~136 metric tons)
  • Price: Cents per bushel
  • Settlement: Cash
  • Termination: Business day preceding 15th of month
  • Currency: USD

3. Options

  • The right (but not obligation) to buy or sell at a specified price on or before a specified date
  • Buyer pays a premium for the right
  • Value influenced by:
    • Underlying asset's market price
    • Length of contract
    • Current volatility of underlying asset
    • Supply and demand
    • Dividend rate of underlying asset

Call Option: Right to buy at a specified price

Put Option: Right to sell at a specified price

Option Example

Google shares trading at $300 per share.

  • You believe shares will increase
  • Purchase a call option with strike price of $315
  • Option expires in one month
  • Premium: $0.37 per contract (usually sold in 100-share lots)
  • Your cash outlay: $37 (for 100 shares)

Scenario: Stock rises to $320

  • You exercise call option, buying at $315
  • Immediately sell at market price of $320
  • Profit per share: $5.00 (sale) minus $0.37 (premium) = $4.63
  • Total profit: $4.63 × 100 shares = $463

Option Risks and Benefits

Buying Call Options

  • Benefit: If stock price rises above strike, you profit
  • Risk: Limited to the premium paid. If stock doesn't rise above strike, you lose only the premium

Selling Call Options (Writing)

  • Benefit: Maximum profit is the premium received
  • Risk: Potentially unlimited. If stock price soars, you must sell at agreed strike price, missing profits

Buying Put Options

  • Used when you believe underlying stock will fall
  • Right to sell shares at option's higher price
  • Can then buy them back at lower market price
  • Risk: Limited to the loss of the premium if option not exercised

Selling Put Options (Writing)

  • Seller believes asset will increase in value
  • Maximum profit: Premium received
  • Loss: Having to buy shares at strike price above current market value

Black-Scholes Option Pricing Model

Key Concept: The Black-Scholes model and its variants assume stock prices follow a lognormal distribution.

Formula Components:

  • C = call option price
  • N = CDF (cumulative distribution function) of normal distribution
  • S_t = spot price of asset
  • K = strike price
  • r = risk-free interest rate
  • t = time to maturity
  • σ (sigma) = volatility of the asset

Important Note: Options with optionality do not react to market prices in a linear manner—both in value and in calculated risk position.

4. Swaps

Definition: A contract to exchange a series of cash flows at specific points in the future based on a specific principal amount.

Key Characteristic: Swaps are zero-sum games—there is always a winner and a loser.

Interest Rate Swaps (IRS)

How They Work: Two parties exchange interest payments on a notional principal amount.

Example Scenario:

  • Notional Value: £1,000,000
  • LIBOR Rate: 5%
  • Bob holds a bond paying LIBOR + 1% (variable rate)
  • Alice holds a bond paying 6% (fixed rate)

Swap Agreement:

  • Bob pays Alice 5.5%
  • Alice pays Bob 6%
  • Net: Alice pays Bob 0.5% ($5,000 annually)

Why Each Party Participates:

  • Bob: Believes LIBOR will decrease. Wants more predictable fixed rate to hedge market risk
  • Alice: Believes LIBOR will increase. Speculator seeking higher returns

Swap Legs: The cash flows exchanged are referred to as "legs"

  • Fixed Leg: Pays fixed interest rate agreed at outset
  • Floating Leg: Linked to future interest rate levels (e.g., LIBOR)

Vanilla Swap: Most common type—floating interest rate exchanged for fixed rate (or vice versa)

Comparative Advantage in Interest Rate Swaps

Example:

  • Company A: Can borrow fixed at 10% or floating at LIBOR + 0.35%
  • Company B: Can borrow fixed at 11.25% or floating at LIBOR + 1.0%

Analysis:

  • Company A has comparative advantage in fixed-rate borrowing (better at 10% vs 11.25%)
  • Company B has comparative advantage in floating-rate borrowing (better at LIBOR + 1.0% vs LIBOR + 0.35%)

Strategy: Each company borrows at its advantage, then they swap via IRS to achieve better rates for both.

5. Swaptions

Definition: A contract giving the right (but not obligation) to enter into a swap agreement at a specific price at a particular point in the future.

Analogy: A swaption is to a swap what an option is to a stock.

Two Types:

  • Payer Swaption: Right to enter swap where you pay fixed rate and receive floating rate
  • Receiver Swaption: Right to enter swap where you receive fixed rate and pay floating rate

Characteristics:

  • OTC (over-the-counter) and unstandardized
  • Terms fully negotiated between buyer and seller
  • Commercial banks are main market makers
  • Only banks can effectively manage portfolios of swaptions

Credit Derivatives: Credit Default Swaps (CDS)

Definition: A contract to insure the credit risk of an underlying bond for an agreed fee over an agreed amount of time.

How a CDS Works:

  • Buyer enters CDS contract and pays premium to seller
  • Seller agrees that upon credit event, they will pay face value plus remaining interest to buyer
  • Contract expires if bond matures without credit event occurring

Types of CDS:

  • Single Name CDS: Protection on one bond issuer
  • Basket CDS: Protection on multiple issuers
  • Index CDS: Protection on index of bonds

CDS Participants

Protection Seller Benefits:

  • Earns investment income with no funding cost
  • Gains customized, synthetic access to risky bond exposure
  • Often protection tenor is shorter than bond tenor, limiting long-term exposure

Protection Buyer Benefits:

  • Hedges default risk on reference asset (bond)
  • Can maintain client relationships while reducing credit risk

CDS Bank Use Case

Scenario: Bank lends £10 million to corporate client

  • Loan rate: LIBOR + 65 basis points (0.65%)
  • Bank buys £10 million default protection at 50 basis points (0.50%)
  • Net benefit: 15 basis points (0.15%) = £15,000 annually
  • Objectives achieved:
    • Maintain relationship with client
    • Reduce credit risk on new loan
    • Generate net revenue

Credit Events Triggering CDS

A CDS is triggered when the following events occur:

  • Bankruptcy: Issuer becomes insolvent or unable to pay debts
  • Failure to Pay: Issuer defaults on debt payments
  • Debt Restructuring: Configuration of debt obligations is changed negatively for credit holder
  • Obligation Acceleration: Debt payment is scheduled before maturity date
  • Repudiation/Moratorium: Issuer refuses to pay principal and interest

Mortgage-Backed Securities (MBS)

Definition: An investment similar to a bond made up of a bundle of home loans bought from the banks that issued them.

How They Work:

  • Investors receive periodic payments similar to bond coupons
  • Payments come from monthly mortgage payments of underlying homeowners
  • Investors are purchasing the cash flow from underlying mortgages

MBS-Specific Risks:

  • Default Risk: Homeowners may default due to job loss, bankruptcy, or other hardships
  • Prepayment Risk: As interest rates decrease, mortgage-owners refinance or prepay. Similar to a callable bond—investors lose higher-rate payments
  • Correlation Risk: If mortgage defaults are highly correlated (geographic area, housing market collapse), diversification benefit is lost
  • Liquidity Risk: MBS investors are primarily institutional. Smaller lots difficult to sell. Large positions difficult to liquidate

Principal-Agent Problem in Lending

Historical Model: Commercial banks underwrote loans and held them until maturity, giving them incentive to minimize credit risk.

Post-1990s Model: Originate-to-Distribute

  • Strong demand from investment banks for mortgage pools enabled originators to resell quickly
  • Originators no longer held credit risk
  • Investment banks purchased mortgage pools based on limited metrics (credit scores, LTV ratio)
  • Problem: Originators became agents of investors, originating loans without caring about credit quality
  • Result: This principal-agent problem encouraged subprime lending
  • Rating agencies underestimated MBS risks

MBS and the 2008 Financial Crisis

Timeline of Crisis:

  • Pre-2006: Rapid increase in home prices caused banks to lower lending standards
  • 2006: Housing prices peaked; quality of all MBS-backed securities declined, ratings became meaningless
  • 2007-2008: Subprime borrowers began defaulting as housing market collapsed
  • 2008: More people "walked away" from mortgages—homes worth less than mortgage owed
  • Result: Large non-payments meant MBS and CDOs greatly overvalued
  • Consequence: Losses piled up. Institutional investors and banks tried and failed to unload bad MBS investments
  • System Risk: Credit tightened; banks at risk of insolvency

Asset-Backed Securities (ABS)

Definition: A security collateralized by a pool of assets (not mortgages).

Examples of Underlying Assets:

  • Credit card debt
  • Home equity loans
  • Student loans
  • Automotive loans
  • Movie revenues
  • Leases and receivables

Securitization Process:

  1. Loan Originators: Create loans (credit card debt, auto loans, etc.)
  2. SPV Creation: Originators sell to SPV (Special Purpose Vehicle)—an orphan company created to spread risks
  3. SPV Function: Provides "remoteness" for originator from liability. SPV sells to trust
  4. Trust: Repackages loans as interest-bearing securities
  5. Investors: Receive cash flows from underlying pool of loans
  6. Tranches: Securities usually issued in different tranches by risk level
  7. Fees: Paid to security providers throughout process

Collateralized Debt Obligations (CDOs)

Definition: Complex asset-backed securities containing mortgage-backed assets, non-mortgage assets, or a combination. Assets become collateral if loans default.

Types:

  • CLO (Collateralized Loan Obligation): Often corporate loans with low credit ratings
  • CBO (Collateralized Bond Obligation): Bond-backed by pool of junk bonds

Tranches: Pieces of the pooled collection of securities, usually debt instruments.

  • Split by risk or other characteristics to make marketable
  • Senior Tranches: Higher credit ratings, lowest yields, repaid first in default
  • Junior Tranches: Lower credit ratings, higher yields, repaid last in default

Structure Benefit: Allows investors of different risk appetites to invest. Senior tranches suitable for conservative investors; junior tranches for risk-seeking investors.

Complexity of CDOs

Structure: CDOs consist of diverse loans and debt instruments repackaged into discrete classes based on credit risk.

Example Structure: An investment bank gathers:

  • Mortgages
  • Corporate bonds
  • Other debt instruments

Then repackages into different tranches with varying risk profiles and returns.

Challenge: The complexity made it difficult for investors and regulators to fully understand underlying risks, contributing to 2008 financial crisis.
