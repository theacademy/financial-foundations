Equity Trading Fundamentals

Learning Objective: Understand the equity trading business, including the process of companies going public, share types, and the fundamentals of stock markets.

What is Equity Trading?

Equity trading involves buying and selling shares of publicly traded companies on stock exchanges. When a company decides to go public through an Initial Public Offering (IPO), its shares become available for trading in the open market. This allows investors to own a piece of the company, and gives the company access to capital from public investors.

The Journey from Private to Public Company

Private Company: A company operates privately with its equity divided among founders, employees, and investors.

IPO (Initial Public Offering): The first sale of shares to the public, where the company lists on a stock exchange. This brings new rules and regulations but also access to public capital markets.

Public Company: The company's shares are now traded publicly, and equity is divided among new shareholders alongside original shareholders.

Market Capitalization (Market Cap)

Definition: A company's total market value, calculated as the stock price multiplied by the number of shares outstanding.

Key Terms:

  • Outstanding Shares: Shares issued by a public company that are freely available to be bought by anyone
  • Restricted Stock: Shares that can only be traded under SEC regulations
  • Floating Stock: Shares currently available to trade

Companies are ranked by market cap into categories:

  • Large Cap: Largest companies, often referred to as "blue chip companies"
  • Mid Cap: Medium-sized companies
  • Small Cap: Smaller companies with greater growth potential

Dividends

Definition: A distribution of a portion of a company's earnings, decided by the board of directors. Dividends can be paid in cash or stock, and are typically paid twice a year.

Why Investors Care About Dividends: Investors invest in equities for two main reasons:

  • Capital appreciation (share price growth)
  • Dividend income (regular cash payments)

Why Companies Pay Dividends: A company with excess cash might choose to distribute it as dividends rather than face acquisition. For example, if a company has $10,000 in the bank but a market cap of only $2,000, it could theoretically be bought and liquidated for profit. Instead, distributing the excess cash as dividends is more attractive to shareholders.

Example: Apple issued a $0.77 dividend on February 13, 2020. An investor who owned 500 shares would have received $385.

Share Classes

Different companies may issue multiple classes of shares with different rights and privileges:

  • Ordinary Shares (Common Stock): Standard shares with dividend and voting rights. Dividend paid after preference shares
  • Preference Shares: Receive dividends ahead of ordinary shares. Entitled to fixed dividend amounts. Often carry no voting rights
  • Deferred Ordinary Shares: Dividend payment deferred until after other share classes receive minimum dividends
  • Non-Voting Ordinary Shares: No voting rights, or voting rights restricted to certain circumstances
  • Redeemable Shares: Company has the option to buy back the shares at a predetermined price

Case Study: Google Share Classes

Google demonstrates a multi-class share structure:

  • Class A Shares (GOOGL): Held by regular investors. One vote per share
  • Class C Shares (GOOG): Held by employees and Class A stockholders. No voting rights
  • Founder Shares (No Symbol): Held by the founders. 10 times the voting power of Class A shares

This structure allows founders to maintain significant control despite public ownership.

Case Study: Snapchat IPO

March 1, 2017 (Day Before IPO): Stock priced at $17 per share. Valuation: $24 billion. Notably, shares carried no voting rights.

March 2, 2017 (Day of IPO): Price soars to $26.05. Stock closes at $24.48—up 44%. Snap valued at $34.56 billion.

March 3, 2017 (Day After IPO): Media commentary:

  • Financial Times: "Snap IPO is the foolish leading the blind"
  • Fortune: "Here's how Snap's IPO just proved we're in a tech bubble"
  • CityAM: "Hold off on criticizing…it may be a Facebook, rather than a Twitter"

The Snapchat IPO illustrates the excitement and skepticism that can surround IPOs, especially in the technology sector.

Stock Exchanges and Market Mechanics

Understanding how stock exchanges work and the mechanics of trading is essential to equity trading.

What Are Stock Exchanges?

Definition: Electronic marketplaces for trading shares and other securities.

Key Characteristics:

  • Stocks are listed on one domestic exchange (though can be listed on other venues)
  • Companies pay listing fees plus annual fees to maintain their listing
  • Exchanges provide regulated, transparent trading environments

Major Stock Exchanges:

  • NYSE (New York Stock Exchange): A physical exchange using an auction market model
  • Nasdaq: Totally electronic. Handles greater daily traded share volume than NYSE

A Day in the Life of Trading

Traditional markets like NYSE operate through distinct phases:

Opening Auction (7:30am EST):

  • Orders can be entered as early as 7:30am (MOO - Market on Open, or LOO - Limit on Open)
  • Reference price is the previous day's closing price
  • Auction opens at 9:30am EST

Continuous Trading (9:30am - 3:58pm EST):

  • Orders are placed onto the orderbook throughout the day
  • Orders are matched based on price and priority
  • Provides continuous price discovery

Closing Auction (3:58pm - 4:00pm EST):

  • MOC (Market on Close) and LOC (Limit on Close) orders can be entered
  • At 3:58pm, orders can no longer be cancelled
  • Market closes at 4:00pm EST

Understanding the Orderbook

What is an Orderbook? A real-time display of all active buy and sell orders for a security on an exchange.

Key Components:

  • Bid: The maximum price a buyer is willing to pay for the stock
  • Ask: The minimum price a seller is willing to accept
  • Spread: The difference between the highest bid and lowest ask (bid-ask spread)
  • MPID: Market Participant ID—identifies who placed the order
  • BBO/NBBO: Best Bid and Offer (Best on one exchange) vs. National Best Bid and Offer (best across all venues)

Example: If the best bid is $93.03 and the best ask is $93.04, the spread is $0.01. This means buyers and sellers are very close on price.

Order Types

Traders use various order types to execute trades:

  • Market Order: Execute immediately at the best available price, regardless of price level
  • Limit Order: Execute only at a specified price or better. Provides price protection but may not execute
  • On Open: Execute in the opening auction
  • On Close: Execute in the closing auction
  • Hidden (Iceberg) Order: Limit order where price and volume are not displayed to other market participants. Only a small portion is visible
  • IOC (Immediate or Cancel): Execute immediately any portion that can be filled, cancel the rest
  • FOK (Fill or Kill): Execute the entire order immediately or cancel it entirely. No partial fills accepted

Liquidity and the Bid-Ask Spread

Liquidity: The ease of converting a security into cash without significantly impacting its price.

Bid-Ask Spread Implications:

  • Narrow spreads = high liquidity, easy to buy/sell at fair prices
  • Wide spreads = low liquidity, difficult to trade without price impact
  • Large institutional trades may temporarily widen spreads due to size

Long and Short Positions

Long Position: Buying shares with the expectation they will increase in value

  • Maximum loss: the amount invested
  • Maximum gain: unlimited (theoretically)

Short Position: Selling borrowed shares with a contract to buy them back later at a lower price

  • Requires a "locate" agreement with a broker to borrow shares
  • Maximum loss: unlimited (price could rise indefinitely)
  • Riskier than long positions

Basis Points and Ticks

Basis Points (BIPs):

  • 1 BIP = 1/100 of a percentage point
  • Example: 5% is 50 BIPs higher than 4.5%
  • 100 BIPs = 1%

Stock Ticks:

  • The smallest valid price change for a stock
  • Example: 1 cent tick (USD), 1 pence tick (GBP)
  • Different securities may have different tick sizes

Market Participants and Market Indices

Understanding the different participants in equity markets and how market indices work is fundamental to equity trading.

Why Invest in Equities?

Investors are drawn to equity markets for several reasons:

  • Regular Income: Dividend payments from profitable companies
  • Capital Growth: Appreciation in share price over time
  • Long Term Investment: Equities are suitable for patient capital
  • High Liquidity: Most public stocks can be bought/sold quickly
  • Transparent Marketplace: Prices and trading information are publicly available
  • Ownership and Voting Rights: Shareholders can influence company decisions

Market Indices

Definition: A hypothetical portfolio of investment holdings that represents a segment of the financial market.

Characteristics:

  • Aggregates the price of several securities
  • Serves as a trend indicator for market sentiment
  • Used as performance benchmarks for investors
  • Help compare portfolio performance against market performance

How Indices Work: An index is calculated from a weighted combination of its constituent stocks. Changes in the index reflect overall market movement in that segment.

ETFs (Exchange-Traded Funds)

Definition: A collection of securities (stocks, bonds, commodities, currencies) bundled into a single financial product and traded like a stock.

Benefits:

  • Instant diversification across many securities
  • Lower fees than active mutual funds
  • Trade like stocks during market hours
  • Transparent holdings and pricing

Example Indices

Nasdaq Indices:

  • NDQ: Top 100 non-financial companies, market-cap weighted
  • NDXE: Top 100 non-financial companies, equally weighted

Regional Indices:

  • MID (S&P): 400 mid-cap companies
  • OMXS30 (Nasdaq Stockholm): 30 most traded Swedish companies

Sector Indices:

  • DRG (Nasdaq): Pharmaceutical companies larger than $75 million market cap

American Depositary Receipts (ADRs)

Definition: Certificates issued by US banks representing shares in foreign companies, allowing investment in non-US companies on US exchanges.

How ADRs Work:

  • A US bank holds the actual foreign shares
  • Issues ADR certificates representing one or more foreign shares
  • ADRs trade on US exchanges like regular stocks

Benefits:

  • Avoids currency exchange issues (dividends converted to USD)
  • No need to establish foreign brokerage accounts
  • Allows foreign companies to attract US investors without US listing

Example: A foreign company's shares on the local exchange can be held by a US bank and represented as ADRs on the NYSE or Nasdaq, allowing US investors to participate.

The Fundamentals of Trading: Buy Low, Sell High

Core Principle: "Buy low and sell high" is one of the most fundamental concepts in stock market investing.

Market Cycles: Stock prices follow consistent patterns around the business cycle and market sentiment:

Bull Market:

  • Prices rising or expected to rise
  • Optimistic times of economic growth
  • Good time to hold or buy quality stocks

Bear Market:

  • Prices falling or expected to fall
  • Times typically shrouded in pessimism
  • Maximum fear can be a buying opportunity

Sentiment and Timing: Times of maximum fear (market crashes) are often good times to buy; times of maximum greed are times to sell.

Algorithmic Trading and Advanced Trading Venues

Modern equity trading relies heavily on technology, algorithms, and multiple trading venues to execute orders efficiently.

Types of Equity Trading

Electronic Trading (E-Trading) - Low Touch:

  • On Exchange Trading: Trading on official stock exchanges
  • High speed (low latency) execution
  • Transparent pricing (orderbook visible)
  • Standardized contracts and rules
  • Facilitates algorithmic trading, HFT, and direct market access

Over-the-Counter Trading (OTC) - High Touch:

  • Agreement to make a trade, often verbally or electronically between parties
  • Off exchange (not on official exchange)
  • May take several days to settle
  • Flexible terms and conditions
  • Facilitates complex, customized deals
  • Less transparency than exchange trading

Smart Order Routing (SOR)

Purpose: Once an algorithm decides to execute an order, the SOR determines the optimal exchange or venue to route the order for best price.

How SOR Works:

  • Monitors liquidity across multiple exchanges
  • Considers dark pools and alternative venues
  • Routes order to venue with best price at that moment
  • Uses consolidated orderbook feed from market data
  • Executes strategies to minimize market impact

Key Consideration: Liquidity varies significantly across different exchanges, so routing to the best venue can result in significant cost savings.

Algorithmic Trading Strategies

Definition: Algorithms allow execution of orders in a programmatic way around variables such as price, timing, and volume.

Common Strategies:

1. VWAP (Volume Weighted Average Price)

  • Uses historical data to calculate average market price over a time period
  • Splits large orders into smaller pieces to minimize market impact
  • Formula: (number of shares bought × share price) / total shares bought
  • Goal: Execute at or better than average price

2. TWAP (Time Weighted Average Price)

  • Splits order evenly over a specified time period
  • Example: spread this order equally over the next two hours
  • Simple but less sophisticated than VWAP

3. POV (Percentage of Volume)

  • Executes a percentage of market volume until order is complete
  • Adaptive to changing market conditions
  • Helps stay "with the market"

4. IS (Implementation Shortfall)

  • Trades more when price is favorable
  • Trades less when price is unfavorable
  • Optimizes timing based on price signals

Requirements: All algorithmic strategies require both historical trading data and live market data to make intelligent execution decisions.

Dark Pools and Crossing Networks

Definition: Private exchanges or forums for trading where the orderbook is not visible to the public.

Why Dark Pools Exist:

  • Cost savings: Avoid exchange fees for internal trades
  • Large orders: Execute big trades without revealing intentions to market
  • Protect trading strategies: Hide order flow from competitors
  • Liquidity: Banks have access to other firms' dark pools

How They Work: If two traders at the same bank (one selling Apple, one buying Apple) match internally, they can trade without going to the exchange and paying fees. This internal crossing network is essentially a dark pool.

Regulatory Note: Dark pools are regulated differently than lit exchanges and have reporting requirements, but offer less transparency to other market participants.

Alternative Trading Systems and Multilateral Trading Facilities

ATS (Alternate Trading System - US Term):

  • A venue to match buy and sell orders
  • Not regulated as an exchange
  • Usually registered as broker-dealers
  • May be LIT (transparent orderbook visible) or DARK (no transparency)

MTF (Multilateral Trading Facility - European Term):

  • European equivalent of ATS
  • Regulated by MIFID II
  • Facilitates exchange of financial instruments between multiple parties
  • May trade instruments without official markets

ECNs/ECNS (Electronic Communication Networks):

  • Alternative name for certain ATS
  • Connect buyers and sellers electronically

Cross Listing and Multiple Venues

Cross Listing: The process of listing a company's shares on multiple exchanges beyond the primary exchange.

Reasons for Cross Listing:

  • Gain investment base in overseas markets
  • Enable 24-hour trading across different time zones
  • Increase liquidity by accessing more investors
  • Raise capital from multiple markets

Trading Implications: Securities may trade on multiple exchanges or ATS simultaneously, requiring smart order routing to find the best price.

High-Frequency Trading and Market Making

Modern equity markets are shaped by high-speed trading technologies and market makers who provide liquidity.

Stock Identifiers

When securities trade electronically, multiple identifiers are used:

  • Ticker/Instrument Code: Main identifier (e.g., AAPL). Doesn't indicate where traded
  • RIC (Reuters Identity Code): Reuters-specific code showing primary listing (e.g., AAPL.N for NYSE)
  • Bloomberg ID: Bloomberg's identifier (e.g., AAPL:US)
  • SEDOL: Stock Exchange Daily Official List. Seven characters (e.g., B0YQ5W0)
  • ISIN: International Securities Identification Number. Twelve characters (e.g., US0378331005)
  • CUSIP: Committee on Uniform Securities Identification Procedures. Nine characters (e.g., 037833100)

Why Multiple Identifiers? Different systems and regions use different identification schemes. Mapping between identifiers is critical for electronic trading.

High-Frequency Trading (HFT)

Definition: An automated trading platform that executes large numbers of orders at extremely high speeds.

Characteristics:

  • Uses algorithms and high-speed computers
  • Orders usually sent as limit orders
  • Orders cancelled immediately if they don't trade
  • Focuses on making thousands of small profits that add up

Low Latency Trading:

  • Technology constantly optimized to reduce decision-making time
  • Reduces time between market data receipt and order execution
  • Measured in microseconds and milliseconds

Co-location (Colos):

  • Some exchanges allow HFT firms to place servers directly next to exchange servers
  • Significantly reduces latency (network delay)
  • Available for a high fee
  • Creates competitive advantage for firms that can afford it

Benefits: Increased market liquidity and tighter bid-ask spreads

Criticisms: May create unfair advantages and potential market instability

Market Making

Definition: A principal trading activity where market makers use their own accounts to continuously quote prices and facilitate trading.

Primary Goal: Profit on the bid-ask spread (buy at bid, sell at ask)

Market Maker Responsibilities:

  • Trade principally (use bank's own capital)
  • Enter and adjust quotes to buy and sell
  • Execute and clear orders on exchanges
  • Commit to continuously quote prices for a set of securities

Value to Markets:

  • Provides continuous liquidity
  • Reduces bid-ask spreads
  • Enables efficient price discovery
  • Facilitates trading at fair prices

Risks: Market makers hold inventory and are exposed to adverse price movements. Large price moves can result in significant losses.

Market Crashes and Circuit Breakers

Understanding Market Crashes: Stock markets can experience dramatic declines due to:

  • Loss of confidence in market fundamentals
  • Systemic financial crises
  • Rapid changes in sentiment
  • Technical or algorithmic failures

Historical Examples:

  • Wall Street Crash (1929): Lasted 12-25 years
  • Black Monday (1987): Lasted 456 trading days
  • Tech Bubble (2000): Lasted 1015 trading days
  • Global Financial Crisis (2008): Lasted 230 trading days
  • Flash Crash (2010): Lasted only 36 minutes (quickly recovered)

Circuit Breakers:

Implemented after Black Monday in 1987, circuit breakers are automatic trading halts designed to curb panic selling.

For Individual Securities: Triggered if price increases or decreases by a certain percentage. Stock is halted from trading for a brief period.

For Market Indices (S&P 500):

  • Level 1 (7% decline): Trading halted for 15 minutes (unless after 3:25pm EST)
  • Level 2 (13% decline): Trading halted for 15 minutes (unless after 3:25pm EST)
  • Level 3 (20% decline): Trading halted for remainder of day

Purpose: Allow market participants time to process information and reduce panic-driven selling. This circuit breaker mechanism has likely prevented even more severe market crashes.

Trading System Architecture

Typical Components:

  • Order Manager: Handles incoming orders and validation
  • Algo Engine: Executes algorithmic trading strategies
  • Smart Order Router (SOR): Routes orders to best venue
  • Exchange Connections: Direct connections to NYSE, Nasdaq, BATS, etc.
  • Crossing Network: Internal dark pool for crossing orders

Reliability and Scale: Trading systems handle millions of orders daily and require:

  • Load balancing across multiple servers
  • Market data replication for high availability
  • Order flow sharding (different instruments on different processors)
  • Redundancy and failover mechanisms

Data Categories in Equity Trading

Client Data: Information about trading clients and their requirements

Reference Data:

  • Stock identifiers (ticker, ISIN, SEDOL, etc.)
  • Index constituents and weightings
  • Rebalancing schedules

Fundamental Data:

  • Company financial information
  • Analyst recommendations (buy, hold, sell)
  • News and events

Corporate Actions Data:

  • Dividends, splits, mergers
  • Expiries (stock options, futures)
  • Triple Witching: Quarterly expiration of stock options, index futures, and index options on the same day (3rd Friday of March, June, September, December)

Market Data:

  • Real-time trades and quotes
  • Order book depth
  • Bid-ask prices
  • Trading volumes

Execution Data:

  • Order fills (executed portions)
  • Order rejections
  • Order cancellations
  • Partial fills

All of this data must be processed in real-time for trading systems to make effective decisions.
