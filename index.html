Introduction to Financial Markets

Learning Objective: Gain a comprehensive understanding of the finance industry and the various business lines that operate within banks, along with the financial products they offer.

The Four Main Types of Banks

The banking industry consists of four primary categories:

  • Central Banks
    Function: Manage the money supply in a single country or series of nations. Set monetary policy goals.
    Examples: US Federal Reserve, European Central Bank
  • Retail Banks
    Function: Offer public financial products and services such as bank accounts and loans to individual consumers.
    Services: Current accounts, savings accounts, mortgages, debit cards, personal loans, online banking
  • Universal Banks
    Function: Provide comprehensive financial services tailored to retail, commercial, and investment services.
    Examples: Deutsche Bank, Bank of America, HSBC
  • Investment Banks
    Function: Act as financial intermediaries performing a variety of services, specializing in large and complex financial transactions as well as acting as brokers or financial advisors for institutional clients.
    Examples: Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley

Bank Basics: The Fractional Reserve System

Banking traditionally involves taking surplus cash from lenders and acting as a financial intermediator to borrowers. The key principle underlying modern banking is the Fractional Reserve System: banks assume that depositors will only want access to a fraction of their deposits at any given time. The remaining funds are invested in money markets.

How it works:

  • Deposit: A sum of money left with a bank (e.g., the bank pays the depositor 3% interest)
  • Loan: A sum of money lent from a bank (e.g., the bank charges a borrower 6% interest)
  • Bank's Profit: The difference between lending rate and borrowing rate (in this example, 3%) is typically invested in money markets

Important consideration: The fractional reserve system assumes that not all depositors will withdraw their funds simultaneously. If this assumption is violated, the bank's reserves may be insufficient to cover all withdrawal requests, potentially leading to a bank run.

Interest Rates and the Interest Rate Spread

Definition: Interest is the percentage of principal charged by the lender for the use of its money.

Example: If you borrow $1,000 at 6% annual interest for one year, you must pay back $1,060 after one year.

The relationship between depositors and borrowers creates the interest rate spread:

  • Depositors receive interest for money saved with banks
  • Banks use this money to lend to borrowers
  • Borrowers pay interest for money lent from banks
  • Banks use loan interest to pay interest to depositors and maintain profitability

Evolution of Modern Banking

Modern banking has evolved significantly from the traditional banking model, introducing key concepts:

  • Money Market Funding: Banks now fund operations through money markets, not just deposits
  • Securitization: Creation of derivative products from banks' own assets or others' assets (loans and mortgages), allowing banks to transfer risk
  • Originate-to-Distribute Model (O2D): A departure from the traditional "Originate-to-Hold" (OTH) model where banks underwrite and hold risk. O2D allows banks to originate loans and immediately sell them, removing underwriting risk and enabling unlimited distribution of products

Historical Context: After the Great Depression in the 1930s, the Glass-Steagall Act (1933) required banks to choose between commercial banking (accepting deposits) or investment banking (market activities). This separation was repealed in 1999, allowing the creation of modern universal banks.

Central Banking and Monetary Policy

Central banks play a crucial role in managing a nation's economy through monetary policy. Understanding their functions and tools is essential to financial literacy.

Primary Functions of Central Banks

Central banks serve as "banks for the banks" and the government. Their key responsibilities include:

  • Holding fractional reserves for commercial banks
  • Providing banking services for the state and other central banks
  • Supervising other banks and the financial system
  • Acting as "lender of last resort" during financial crises
  • Managing the money supply and implementing monetary policy
  • Setting interest rates to influence economic activity

Major Central Banks: Bank of England, Federal Reserve System (US), Bank of Canada, European Central Bank, Hong Kong Monetary Authority

Interest Rates, Inflation, and Monetary Policy

Key Definitions:

Interest Rates: The percentage of principal charged by the lender for the use of its money.

Inflation: A quantitative measure of the rate at which the average price level of selected goods and services in an economy increases over time. It indicates a decrease in the purchasing power of a nation's currency. Measured by CPI (Consumer Price Index) and PPI (Producer Price Index).

The Central Bank's Interest Rate Tool

Central banks influence inflation by adjusting interest rates (they typically have a target inflation rate of around 2%):

Raising Interest Rates:

  • Encourages saving (higher returns on savings accounts and bonds)
  • Decreases inflation (reduces spending and demand)
  • Increases currency demand (higher interest rates attract foreign investment)
  • Leads to higher exchange rates

Lowering Interest Rates:

  • Encourages spending and borrowing
  • Increases inflation
  • Decreases currency demand
  • Lowers exchange rates

The Relationship Between Exchange Rates and Inflation

A higher interest rate should lead to a higher exchange rate, which helps reduce inflationary pressure by:

  • Making imports cheaper (lower prices of imported goods)
  • Reducing demand for exports
  • Increasing incentive for exporters to cut costs

Quantitative Easing (QE)

What is Quantitative Easing? When interest rates are very low and inflation needs boosting through spending, central banks may employ Quantitative Easing‚Äîthe creation of new money electronically to purchase government bonds or other assets.

How it works:

  • The central bank creates new money electronically
  • This money is used to purchase government bonds or other assets
  • Increased bond prices reduce yields, making bonds less attractive
  • Investors seek returns elsewhere, freed-up money flows into lending
  • Increased lending supply lowers the cost of borrowing
  • Result: stimulated spending and economic growth

Reversing QE: If inflation becomes too high, the central bank can sell previously purchased assets to reduce the amount of money and spending in the economy, bringing inflation back to target levels.

Historical Example: Quantitative Easing was first used in the UK in 2009. The objective is to meet the government's inflation target while maintaining economic stability.

Banking Sectors and Financial Services

Different banking sectors serve different client needs with specialized products and services.

Retail Banking

Definition: Banking for individual consumers offering traditional banking products and services.

Products and Services:

  • Current/Checking Accounts
  • Savings Accounts
  • Mortgages
  • Personal Loans
  • Debit Cards
  • Checks
  • Money Transfers
  • Overdraft Facilities
  • Online Banking
  • Telephone Banking
  • Physical Branch Access
  • Payment Processing

Monetary Policy in Retail Banking

Retail banks can set their own lending and borrowing rates for individual clients. However, the rate at which banks lend to each other (the interbank rate) is typically influenced by central bank policy.

LIBOR (London Interbank Offered Rate): The benchmark interest rate at which major global banks lend to one another in the international interbank market for short-term loans. LIBOR is calculated and published daily by the Intercontinental Exchange (ICE).

Example of Rate Setting:

  • Barclays borrows at 1.33% (set by Barclays' lending partner)
  • Barclays lends to HSBC at 0.93% (set by Barclays)
  • Barclays borrows from HSBC at 0.91% (set by HSBC)
  • LIBOR average: 0.92% (published benchmark)
  • Barclays lends to retail customers at 5.5% (set by Barclays)

Corporate Banking

Definition: Banking services tailored for businesses of various sizes, offering retail banking services plus specialized corporate financial solutions.

Products and Services:

  • Business accounts and payment processing
  • Loans and credit products
  • Treasury and cash management services (e.g., managing payment flows for large retailers and marketplaces)
  • Equipment lending and leasing
  • Commercial real estate financing
  • Trade finance and international payment facilitation
  • Employer services and payroll management

Private Banking and Wealth Management

Definition: Specialized banking services for high net worth individuals with more than $1 million of liquid assets (assets that can be converted to cash quickly, such as cash or shares).

Typical Clients:

  • Entrepreneurs
  • Executives and corporate leaders
  • Landowners with significant real estate holdings
  • Celebrities
  • Royalty

Services Beyond Standard Retail Banking:

  • Personalized financial advice and relationship management
  • Investment planning and portfolio management
  • Wealth structuring and succession planning
  • Tax planning and optimization strategies
  • Discretionary services and privacy protection

Investment Banking

Definition: A financial intermediary that performs a variety of services specializing in large and complex financial transactions and acting as a broker or financial advisor for institutional clients.

Primary Clients: Corporations, governments, institutional investors (investment funds, pension funds, hedge funds), and other large financial institutions.

Core Services:

  • Mergers and Acquisitions (M&A): Advisory and facilitation of company mergers and acquisitions
  • Financing: Capital raising through various instruments (syndicated loans, bond and securities issuance, project financing)
  • Security Issuance & Underwriting: Facilitating IPOs and new bond issuances
  • Company Restructuring: Advising on restructurings and sales of subsidiaries
  • Research and Advisory: Often provided free of charge to build client relationships and encourage use of fee-based services
  • Brokerage and Market Making: Facilitating securities trading and providing market liquidity

Why Companies Pursue Mergers and Acquisitions

Strategic reasons for mergers include:

  • Generate profits and shareholder value
  • Achieve economies of scale, especially in areas such as research and development
  • Expand market share and competitive positioning
  • Gain access to new technologies, products, or markets

Historical Example: Bank of New York and Mellon Financial Corporation combined to form BNY Mellon, a major global financial services company.

Capital Markets and Investment Banking Operations

Capital markets are the backbone of modern finance, enabling the flow of capital from savers to borrowers and investors.

What Are Capital Markets?

Definition: The system of markets where securities (stocks and bonds) are issued and traded, allowing companies and governments to raise medium- and long-term financing.

Investment banks facilitate the operation of capital markets alongside exchanges, brokers, interdealer brokers, and market data providers. Trading has evolved from traditional open-outcry systems to electronic trading platforms.

Primary vs. Secondary Markets

Primary Market: New Security Issuance

  • The market where new securities are issued for the first time
  • Allows companies and governments to raise capital directly
  • Includes IPOs (Initial Public Offerings), new bond issuances, and new share offerings
  • Raises medium- and long-term financing for capital projects

Secondary Market: Buying and Selling of Existing Securities

  • The market where previously issued securities are bought and sold among investors
  • Provides liquidity, allowing investors to exit positions
  • Price discovery through continuous trading activity
  • Dominated by electronic trading systems

Secondary Market Services: Market Making and Brokerage

Market Making - Dealers: Market makers provide liquidity by constantly buying and selling securities, profiting from the spread between bid and ask prices. They maintain inventory and absorb temporary supply/demand imbalances.

Brokerage - Brokers: Brokers facilitate transactions between buyers and sellers, earning commissions without taking principal risk. They act as intermediaries matching orders.

M&A and Financing Services

Mergers and Acquisitions: Investment banks play a central role in facilitating mergers and acquisitions by providing:

  • Strategic advisory and valuation expertise
  • Due diligence support
  • Deal structuring and negotiation facilitation
  • Financing and underwriting support

Financing Solutions: When clients need to raise capital, investment banks offer:

  • Strategic advisory on optimal financing structure
  • Investor introductions and management
  • Syndicated loans (spreading loan participation among multiple lenders to manage risk)
  • Bond and securities issuance (IPOs, corporate bonds, structured products)
  • Project financing for major infrastructure and corporate projects

Investment Banking Organization: Front Office, Middle Office, and Back Office

Investment banks are typically organized into three main functional areas:

Front Office: Client-facing revenue-generating activities including:

  • Proprietary Trading: Trading the firm's own capital (nostro accounts) to generate profits
  • Sales and Trading: Selling securities to clients and making markets
  • Investment Banking Advisory: M&A and corporate finance advisory
  • Research: Equity research and strategic analysis

Middle Office: Risk management and compliance functions

Back Office: Operations and settlement functions including:

  • Trade settlement and clearing
  • Netting (offsetting positions across multiple transactions)
  • Regulatory compliance and documentation
  • System operations and infrastructure

Note on Separation: Strict "Chinese walls" (information barriers) exist between research, brokerage, and proprietary trading to prevent conflicts of interest and insider trading.

The 24-Hour Banking Day: Follow the Sun

Global financial markets operate continuously across time zones:

  • Tokyo Market Open: 00:00 GMT / 09:00 JST
  • Hong Kong Market Open: 01:30 GMT / 09:30 HKT
  • London/Euro Markets Open: 08:00 GMT
  • US Markets Open: 14:30 GMT / 09:30 ET

Many global banks operate on a "Follow the Sun" support model, maintaining operations across multiple time zones to serve clients in Asia, Europe, and North America throughout the 24-hour trading cycle. Some business areas operate 24/7 (such as online banking), introducing continuous system availability requirements.

Note: Daylight savings time adjustments affect these opening times seasonally.

Risk Management and Market Operations

Understanding risk is fundamental to investment banking and financial decision-making.

Diversification: A Core Risk Management Strategy

Definition: Diversification is a risk management strategy that involves mixing different types of investments within a portfolio to limit exposure to any single asset or risk factor.

Key Principle: All investments involve some level of risk. Diversification reduces overall portfolio risk without necessarily reducing expected returns.

Risk Appetite: Both individual investors and financial institutions have specific risk appetites‚Äîthe level of risk they are willing to tolerate to achieve their financial objectives. Understanding and managing risk appetite is critical to sound investment decisions.

Market Risk: The Four Standard Market Risk Factors

What is Market Risk? Market risk is the risk that movements in market prices lead to losses. It arises from four primary sources:

  • Price Risk: Prices rise and fall due to market sentiment and macroeconomic factors. Securities held in inventory may decline in value.
  • Interest Rate Risk: Changes in interest rates affect bond prices and discount rates used in valuation models.
  • Currency/Foreign Exchange Risk: Exchange rates fluctuate, affecting the value of foreign currency holdings and international transactions.
  • Commodity Price Risk: Commodity prices (oil, metals, agricultural products) fluctuate based on supply and demand dynamics.

Hedging Against Market Risk

Definition: Hedging is the use of derivative instruments or offsetting positions to mitigate against adverse price movements and market risk.

Measurement of Market Risk: Market risk is measured using various quantitative techniques, including:

  • Value-at-Risk (VaR): A statistical measure estimating the maximum potential loss under normal market conditions over a specific time period at a given confidence level
  • Stress Testing: Simulating extreme market scenarios to assess portfolio resilience
  • Scenario Analysis: Evaluating portfolio performance under various hypothetical market conditions

Investment Banking Trade Life Cycle

A typical trade undergoes several stages from conception through settlement:

  • Initiation: Front office identifies a trading opportunity or receives a client request
  • Deal Structure: Terms and structure are determined
  • Execution: Trade is executed in the market
  • Netting: Offsetting positions across multiple transactions are netted (e.g., bought 100 shares of Apple and sold 50 in two different transactions = net long 50 shares)
  • Confirmation: Middle office confirms trade details with counterparties
  • Settlement: Back office settles payment and delivery
  • Risk Management: Ongoing monitoring of market risk and collateral positions

Types of Asset Classes

Investment banks deal in multiple asset classes, each with distinct characteristics:

  • Equities: Ownership stakes in companies (stocks and share derivatives)
  • Fixed Income/Bonds: Debt securities issued by corporations and governments
  • Foreign Exchange (FX): Trading in currencies and exchange rates
  • Commodities: Physical goods such as metals, energy products, and agricultural products
  • Derivatives: Financial contracts whose value is derived from underlying assets (options, futures, swaps, forwards)
  • Structured Products: Complex securities combining multiple underlying assets and derivatives

Risk Considerations: Each asset class has different risk-return characteristics. Understanding these differences is essential for portfolio construction and risk management.
