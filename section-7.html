Financial Regulation and Risk Management

Learning Objective: Understand regulatory frameworks governing financial markets, types of risks banks face, and regulatory approaches to risk management.

Global Regulatory Framework

International Coordination: Financial regulation is coordinated globally across major economic centers.

Canada

  • Bank of Canada (BoC) – Central bank
  • Office of the Superintendent of Financial Institutions (OSFI) – Banking regulator

United Kingdom

  • Bank of England (BoE) – Central bank
  • Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) – Conduct and market regulator
  • Prudential Regulation Authority (PRA) – Prudential/safety regulator

United States

  • Federal Reserve System (The Fed) – Central bank
  • Securities Exchange Commission (SEC) – Securities regulator
  • Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC) – Derivatives regulator

European Union

  • European Central Bank (ECB) – Central bank
  • European Banking Authority (EBA) – Banking regulator
  • European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) – Securities regulator

Hong Kong

  • Securities & Futures Commission (SFC) – Securities regulator
  • Hong Kong Monetary Authority (HKMA) – Central bank

Regulator Objectives

Common Goals Across Regulators:

  • Systemic stability (prevent financial system collapse)
  • Consumer/investor protection (ensure fair treatment)
  • Market transparency (ensure informed decision-making)
  • Anti-fraud and market abuse prevention
  • Financial crime prevention (money laundering, terrorist financing)

The Scale of Modern Regulation

Current Era: We are now in an era of maximum regulation.

Regulatory Expansion: Since the 2008 financial crisis, regulatory requirements have expanded dramatically:

  • New agencies created (FSOC in US, ESMA in EU, PRA in UK)
  • Capital requirements increased (Basel III)
  • Liquidity requirements added (LCR, NSFR)
  • Stress testing mandated
  • Derivatives regulation expanded (EMIR, MiFID II, Dodd-Frank)
  • Conduct rules tightened

Layers of Regulation

Multiple Jurisdictions: Regulation can be applied at multiple levels, and all rules must be complied with:

  • International Level: Basel Committee standards, IOSCO principles
  • Regional Level: EU directives, regional regulatory bodies
  • National Level: National regulators (Fed, FCA, etc.)
  • Local Level: State or provincial regulations

Complexity: A global bank must comply with regulations from multiple jurisdictions simultaneously, often with conflicting rules.

Recent Regulatory Timeline

Key Regulations Introduced (2010-2019):

  • 2010: FATCA (Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act)
  • 2010: Dodd-Frank Legislation (US)
  • 2011: AIFMD (Alternative Investment Fund Managers Directive)
  • 2012: Financial Services Act (UK)
  • 2013: Capital Requirements Directive IV (Europe)
  • 2014: EMIR Transaction Reporting
  • 2016: Money Market Fund Reform
  • 2017: Volcker Rule (enforcement)
  • 2018: MiFID II / MIFIR (Markets in Financial Instruments)
  • 2019: Basel III / Ring Fencing (separation of banking)

Value at Risk (VaR)

Definition: The loss in value of a portfolio that can be expected over a given period of time (e.g., 1 day) with a probability not exceeding a given number (e.g., 5%).

Mathematical Expression: P(Portfolio Loss ≤ VaR) = K, where K = given probability

Example: "We expect that our worst daily loss should not exceed 5% on 99% of occasions if we invest £5,000 in this asset."

Key Considerations in VaR:

  • Time period holding the asset (1-day, 10-day, etc.)
  • Confidence level in the asset (95%, 99%, etc.)
  • Loss amount ($1 million, £10 million, etc.)

VaR in Market Risk Capital Requirements

Regulatory Formula:

  • Regulatory Capital = Market Risk Capital + Specific Risk Capital + Counterparty Risk Capital
  • Market Risk Capital = Max [Average of 10-Day 99% VaR × Multiplier, yesterday's 10-Day 99% VaR]
  • Multiplier is >3, depending on regulatory requirement

Data Requirements: Regulators require at least one year of historical data.

Calculation Methods: Banks can use three methods to calculate VaR:

  • Historical Simulation: Use past price movements to estimate future outcomes
  • Variance/Covariance Method: Assume normal distribution, use correlation and volatility
  • Monte Carlo Simulation: Random simulation of many scenarios

Credit Risk

Definition: The risk of a debt issuer defaulting on a credit obligation.

Consequences of Default:

  • Loss of principal investment
  • Loss of expected cash flows
  • Potential recovery of partial value (collateral, bankruptcy proceedings)

Mitigation Strategies:

  • Upfront assessment of credit risk of debt issuers
  • Continual monitoring of credit quality
  • Diversification across counterparties
  • Collateral requirements
  • Credit derivatives (CDS)

Examples of Credit Risk:

  • Company unable to repay asset-secured debt (e.g., equipment loan)
  • Bond issuer failing to pay coupon or principal when due
  • Consumer defaulting on mortgage, credit card, or personal loan

Credit Rating Agencies

Purpose: Rate the creditworthiness of companies, products, and countries. They answer: "How likely are they to repay their loan?"

Examples of Ratings:

  • USA Sovereign Bond: Aaa (Moody's) / AAA (S&P), Stable outlook
  • UK Sovereign: Aa / AA, Stable outlook
  • Greek Government: BB- / BB, Positive outlook (recovering from crisis)
  • Mexico Sovereign: BBB+, Negative outlook

Rating Scale:

  • AAA/Aaa: Highest quality, lowest risk
  • AA/Aa: Very high quality
  • A: High quality
  • BBB/Baa: Investment grade
  • BB/Ba: Non-investment grade (high yield)
  • B: More speculative
  • CCC/Caa: Substantial risks
  • CC: Extremely speculative

Major Agencies: Moody's, S&P (Standard & Poor's), Fitch

Financial Statements and Financial Analysis

Purpose: Public companies issue annual and interim financial statements showing the health of a company.

Components:

  • Income Statement: Shows revenue, expenses, and profits
  • Balance Sheet: Shows assets, liabilities, and equity
  • Cash Flow Statement: Shows movement of cash in and out

Key Metrics from Financial Statements:

  • Revenue: Money coming in
  • Expenses: Money going out (not including tax, interest, depreciation, amortization)
  • Gross Profit: Revenue minus expenses
  • Net Profit: Revenue minus all expenses, interest, taxes, depreciation, amortization
  • EBITDA: Earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, amortization (often used for comparisons)

Why Financial Statements Matter:

  • Help companies make purchasing and corporate decisions
  • Help investors evaluate company value
  • Help regulators ensure compliance (prevent money laundering, etc.)

Asset Depreciation

Concept: The cost of a depreciating asset is divided across multiple income statements for the asset's useful life.

Depreciating Assets: Machinery, buildings, computers, vehicles, furniture, equipment, fixtures

Why Important:

  • Cost not captured in single year's expenses (non-cash expense)
  • Allows cost to be divided over multiple statements
  • Matches cost of asset with revenue it generates
  • Makes balance sheets healthier by diluting large asset values

Example: Laptop purchased in 2014

  • Cost: £1,000
  • Expected life: 5 years
  • Salvage value: £250
  • Annual depreciation: (£1,000 - £250) / 5 = £150/year
  • Book value over time: £1,000 (2014) → £850 (2015) → £700 (2016) → £550 (2017) → £400 (2018) → £250 (2019)

Company Share Metrics

Earnings Per Share (EPS): Portion of company's profits allocated to each outstanding share.

Example:

  • Company ABC net income: £50 million
  • Outstanding shares: 14 million
  • Dividend paid: £0.50/share (£7 million total)
  • EPS = £50m / 14m = £3.07

Price-to-Earnings Ratio (P/E): Stock price divided by earnings per share.

Example:

  • Stock price: £22.24
  • EPS: £3.07
  • P/E Ratio: 7.24
  • Interpretation: Investors pay £7.24 for every £1 of earnings

What P/E Indicates:

  • High P/E: Investors expect higher future earnings growth
  • Low P/E: Company may be undervalued or outperforming expectations

Return on Investment (ROI): Measures profitability of an investment.

Example:

  • Initial investment: £1,000
  • Value after 2 years: £1,250
  • Profit: £250
  • ROI: 25%

Uses: Assess profitability of past expenditure, potential profitability of future investments, investment worthiness, investment comparisons

Basel Framework

Basel Committee on Banking Supervision (BCBS)

  • Formed in 1974 by central bank governors
  • Original purpose: Enhance financial stability by improving banking supervision
  • Current membership: 45 members from 28 countries

Capital Adequacy: Minimum capital reserves a bank must maintain to absorb losses. Acts as protection for depositors.

Basel I (1988)

Requirement: 8% ratio of capital to risk-weighted assets

Risk Weights:

  • 0% Risk: Cash, central bank reserves, government debt
  • 10%: Public sector debt
  • 20%: Bank debt
  • 50%: Residential mortgages
  • 100% Risk: Private sector debt, real estate, equipment, capital instruments

Formula: Capital (Tier 1 or 2) ≥ 8% × Risk Weighted Assets

Adoption: Standards adopted by 120+ countries

Limitations:

  • Broad risk weights are blunt instruments
  • Doesn't account for newer securitized products and derivatives
  • Doesn't account for diversification as risk management strategy
  • Difficult for regulators to assess with opaque portfolio views
  • Doesn't consider market risk

Basel I: Market Risk Amendment (1996)

Addition: Amendment to account for market risk in capital requirements

Key Provision: Addresses risks from trading activities vs. holding loans

Calculation Methods:

  • Calculate VaR daily
  • Or use "building block" approach: Calculate general market risk and specific security risk separately, then combine

Basel II (2004)

Structure: 3-pillar framework to replace original accord

Pillar 1: Minimum Capital Requirements

  • Credit risk capital
  • Market risk capital (from 1996 amendment)
  • Operational risk capital (new)

Pillar 2: Supervisory Review and Internal Risk Assessment

  • Regulators review bank's risk management
  • Banks assess their own risk profiles
  • Stress testing requirements

Pillar 3: Market Discipline via Disclosure

  • Public disclosure of risks
  • Transparency requirements
  • Market enforces discipline through pricing

Credit Risk Innovation: Banks could use external credit ratings or internal risk categorizations for capital calculations.

Limitation: When Lehman Brothers failed in 2008, it became clear Basel II was inadequate. Basel committee noted:

  • Lehman had too much leverage
  • Inadequate liquidity buffers
  • Poor governance and risk management
  • Inappropriate incentive structures

Basel III (2010+)

Response: Created in response to 2008 financial crisis to strengthen capital framework

Key Enhancements:

  • Higher capital requirements overall
  • Higher quality capital required
  • Liquidity requirements added (LCR, NSFR)
  • Leverage ratio requirement
  • Countercyclical capital buffer
  • Systemically important bank surcharges

MiFID and ESMA

MiFID I (2004)

  • Markets in Financial Instruments Directive
  • Applicable to European banks since 2007
  • Primarily focused on equities
  • Aim: Facilitate cross-border trading within EU
  • Covers: Business conduct, authorization requirements, regulatory reporting, trade transparency
  • Limitation: Became inadequate after 2008 crisis

ESMA (European Securities and Markets Authority)

  • Began operating in 2009
  • Independent EU authority accountable to European Parliament
  • 3 main objectives:
    • Investor protection
    • Orderly markets
    • Financial stability

MiFID II (2018)

  • Revised Markets in Financial Instruments Directive
  • New Markets in Financial Instruments Regulation (MIFIR)
  • More ambitious than MiFID I
  • Effective January 3, 2018

MiFID II Coverage:

  • OTF (Organized Trading Facility) – captures unregulated trades on non-regulated platforms
  • Increased transparency, pre- and post-trade
  • Commodity derivative position limits (reduce speculation)
  • Rules for algorithmic and high-frequency trading
  • Increased investor protection requirements
  • Expanded transaction reporting

EMIR Reporting

European Market Infrastructure Regulation (2012)

Applies to: All types of derivative contracts (interest rates, FX, credit, equities, commodities)

Requirements: Parties in ANY derivative contract must:

  • Report every contract to a trade repository (TR)
  • Clear applicable trades via a central counterparty (CCP)
  • Implement new risk management standards for bilateral OTC derivatives not cleared

Identifiers:

  • LEI (Legal Entity Identifier): Identifies companies in financial transactions
  • UTI (Unique Trade Identifier): Identifies each derivative trade

Central Counterparty Clearing

Counterparty Credit Risk: In every trade, both parties risk the other party defaulting.

Solution: Central Counterparty (CCP)

  • CCP sits between two parties of trade
  • Acts as trade guarantor
  • Example: CME Group

Key Benefit: Multilateral Netting

  • Consolidates all transactions with a client into one transaction
  • Simplifies outstanding exposures
  • Reduces complexity of bilateral trades
  • Reduces settlement risk

FATCA (Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act)

Year: 2010

Purpose: Promote cross-border tax compliance, reveal US persons' foreign accounts

How it Works:

  • Implements Common Reporting Standard (CRS) for automatic exchange of information
  • At least 96 countries share information on residents' assets and incomes
  • Tax withholding can be used if reporting not provided

Impact: Biggest burden on Foreign Financial Institutions (FFIs)

The Dodd-Frank Act

Year: 2010 – The Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act

Purpose: Decrease risk in US financial system in response to 2008 crisis

Scope: Approximately 2,300 pages of provisions phased in over multiple years

New Agencies Established: Created several new agencies to oversee financial system and prevent future crises

The Volcker Rule

Definition: Part of Dodd-Frank, named after former Federal Reserve Chairman Paul Volcker

Purpose: Prevent banks from acting like hedge funds

Prohibition: Banks cannot trade with their own funds (proprietary trading) for their own profits

Also Prohibits: Banks from owning hedge funds or private equity funds

Rationale: Stemmed from banks taking on complex, high-risk positions and losing. Example: Bear Stearns' hedge fund division made bad real estate bets, leading to bank collapse in 2008.

Compliance in Banks

Role of Compliance: Translate new rules into procedures and advise business. Each business area has dedicated compliance team.

Compliance Responsibilities:

  • Monitoring: Monitor deal sizes, market impact, counterparties involved, trading mandates, recording agreements
  • Control: Establish controls to prevent rule violations
  • Checking: Verify compliance with all rules and procedures

Example - Sales and Trading Compliance:

  • Monitor deal sizes and market impact
  • Verify counterparties approved for trading
  • Ensure trading within authorized mandates
  • Record all client agreements
  • Monitor electronic trading systems for violations

Key Takeaways

  • Regulation is extensive and constantly evolving
  • Multiple layers of regulation must be complied with
  • Basel framework forms foundation of capital requirements
  • Risk management is critical—VaR, stress testing, credit analysis
  • Regulatory compliance is every employee's responsibility
  • Future regulations will likely continue to expand
