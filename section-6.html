Treasury Operations and Bank Liquidity Management

Learning Objective: Understand the treasury business function in a bank, including how banks manage their own capital, liquidity, and funding in a global environment.

What is Treasury?

Core Function: The treasury department controls and manages the bank's own money.

Operating Environment: Treasury operates in a global environment characterized by:

  • Market volatility
  • Ever-increasing regulation
  • Multiple currencies and time zones
  • Complex funding requirements

Key Responsibilities: Treasury manages three critical functions:

  • Funding: Ensuring the bank has sufficient capital to operate
  • Liquidity: Ensuring day-to-day payment obligations can be met
  • Capital Management: Optimizing returns while meeting regulatory requirements

Capital Management

Objective: Structure the bank's funds to ensure returns are maximized whilst meeting regulatory standards.

Investment Profile Timelines: Treasury manages funds across different time horizons:

  • Short-Term: Days to weeks. Highest liquidity required
  • Mid-Term: Weeks to months. Moderate liquidity
  • Long-Term: Years. Lower liquidity needs, potential for higher returns

Global Currency Positioning: Global banks position themselves in multiple currencies to:

  • Efficiently fund transactions across the bank in those currencies
  • Benefit from currency movements
  • Reduce overall funding costs
  • Support international client operations

Treasury Stakeholders

Internal Stakeholders:

Investors

  • Treasury must advertise strength and stability of the company
  • Capital management decisions impact stock price and investor confidence

Regulators

  • Treasury must comply with required regulatory standards
  • Capital ratios, liquidity requirements, stress tests

Finance Department

  • Need to know end-to-end cost of every transaction
  • Treasury provides funding cost allocation

Trading Desks

  • Need to understand implications of trades on bank's capital
  • Treasury advises on capital impact of trading activity

Risk Department

  • Requires development of stress-testing models
  • Treasury provides inputs for risk calculations

Technology Department

  • Needs data for auto-calculation and distribution of recharge metrics
  • Treasury provides funding cost data to systems

Execution Services

Purpose: Treasury manages the bank's requirement to execute transactions in multiple currencies.

Typical Scenario:

  • Front office team (e.g., capital financing) agrees to business loan in euros
  • Counterparty needs euros purchased on the money market
  • Treasury execution services facilitate the transaction
  • Treasury advises on cost (what exchange rate to offer)

Example Transaction:

  • Bank needs $300 million USD
  • Treasury determines: "How many £ will it take to buy $300 million today?"
  • Based on current FX rates and market conditions
  • Treasury executes the transaction efficiently

Interbank Lending

What is Interbank Lending? Banks lend assets to each other for specified periods, usually short-term or overnight.

How It Works:

  • Bank A needs liquidity, borrows from Bank B
  • Interest is calculated using the interbank rate
  • Loan period can be as short as one day (overnight)
  • Repayment at end of loan period plus interest

Interbank Rates:

  • The rate at which banks can borrow from each other
  • Driven by availability of money in the market
  • More lenders in market = lower rates
  • Fewer lenders in market = higher rates
  • Common interbank rates include ICE LIBOR, EURIBOR, US Dollar LIBOR

Credit Ratings Impact: Banks have credit ratings that impact the rate they pay for borrowing.

  • Higher-rated banks pay lower rates
  • Lower-rated banks pay higher rates
  • Similar to corporate bond spreads

LIBOR – London Interbank Offered Rate

Definition: A benchmark rate for currency borrowing.

How LIBOR is Calculated:

  • Daily rate submissions from 11-17 contributor banks
  • Each bank answers: "At what rate could you borrow funds in a reasonable market size just prior to 11am London time?"
  • The rate is intended to be representative of average rate a bank could borrow at in London money market
  • Rates published by ICE (Intercontinental Exchange)

LIBOR Quotation: Annualized interest rate

  • Example: 1-month GBP LIBOR at 2.00000% = 2% divided by 365 days
  • Available in multiple currencies (GBP, USD, EUR, JPY, CHF, etc.)
  • Available in multiple tenors (overnight, 1-week, 1-month, 3-months, 6-months, 1-year)

LIBOR Uses:

  • Pricing benchmark for bank loans
  • Reference rate for interest rate derivatives
  • Basis for interbank lending rates
  • Used in mortgages ("Loan at LIBOR + 2.5%")

LIBOR History and Scandals

Pre-2012: Problems with LIBOR

  • Little regulatory oversight of rate submissions
  • Traders submitted rates that would benefit their trading positions
  • Rates were supposed to reflect actual rates banks could borrow at
  • Instead, rates submitted were sometimes designed to profit rate swap traders
  • Collusion occurred across different banks to collectively adjust rates

2012: LIBOR Rigging Scandal

  • Discovered that multiple banks had manipulated LIBOR submissions
  • Massive fines imposed on banks involved
  • Criminal prosecutions launched
  • Result: Loss of confidence in LIBOR

Post-2012: New Governance Structure (ICE LIBOR)

  • Rates now based on actual transactions with records kept
  • Criminal sanctions in place to enable prosecution for manipulation
  • Increased regulatory oversight and verification
  • Better methodology to prevent manipulation

Ongoing Transition: Financial industry transitioning away from LIBOR to new benchmark rates (SOFR in US, SONIA in UK) due to persistent sustainability concerns.

Funding

Objective: Funding teams ensure the investment bank has sufficient liquidity to meet payment obligations on a daily basis.

Key Requirement: Sufficient cash must be available in the correct currencies in all required locations to ensure trades can be settled as agreed.

Liquidity Holdings: Banks hold a proportion of highly liquid assets to meet short-term liabilities:

  • Cash (currency)
  • FX swaps (can convert to other currencies quickly)
  • Overnight deposits (can withdraw next day)
  • Government securities (easily traded)
  • Central bank facilities

Daily Liquidity Management: Treasury must forecast:

  • Expected inflows and outflows each day
  • Minimum liquidity cushion needed
  • Optimal currency mix
  • Timing of funding activities

Funding Sources:

  • Customer deposits
  • Wholesale funding (bonds, CDs)
  • Interbank lending
  • Central bank facilities (repos, discount window)
  • Asset sales (if needed)

Stress Testing

Definition: Assessment of how well a bank can withstand market shocks and liquidity disruption.

Historical Context: After 2008 financial crisis, banks became subject to mandatory stress testing.

Regulatory Stress Tests:

  • Bank of England (BoE): Introduced stress testing of UK banks in 2014
  • Federal Reserve (US): Stress testing as part of Dodd Frank regulation
  • Other Regulators: Most major financial regulators now require stress tests

Stress Test Scenarios: Banks model performance under hypothetical adverse conditions:

  • Severe economic recession
  • Market volatility and credit crises
  • Funding market disruption
  • Commodity price shocks
  • Interest rate spikes or crashes
  • Counterparty defaults

Capital Ratios

Core Tier 1 Capital: Shareholders' equity and retained earnings

  • Most important capital measure
  • The "purest" form of capital
  • Highest-quality loss-absorbing capacity

Core Tier 1 Capital Ratio: The ratio of a bank's core equity capital to its total risk-weighted assets.

BoE Hurdle Rate (2014): Minimum Core Tier 1 Capital ratio of 4.5%

  • Later adjusted (2016) to reflect differences required by globally systemically important banks
  • Systemically important banks required to hold more capital

Regulatory Minimum: Banks must maintain capital above regulatory minimums at all times.

Stress Test Results: Must demonstrate adequate capital even under severe stress scenarios.

Treasury's Role in Risk Management

Market Risk Management:

  • Monitor foreign exchange exposure
  • Hedge interest rate risk
  • Manage liquidity risk
  • Monitor credit risk from counterparties

Regulatory Compliance:

  • Maintain minimum capital ratios
  • Meet liquidity coverage ratios (LCR)
  • Monitor loan-to-deposit ratios
  • Comply with concentration limits

Performance Monitoring:

  • Track funding costs
  • Monitor return on capital
  • Measure internal transfer pricing accuracy
  • Report to management and regulators

Treasury Technology

Systems Required:

  • Cash management systems (track inflows/outflows)
  • Treasury management systems (optimize funding)
  • Risk measurement systems (VaR, stress testing)
  • Market data feeds (FX rates, interest rates)
  • Regulatory reporting systems

Real-Time Management: Treasury often operates 24/5 (Monday-Friday, around the clock) to manage global funding needs across time zones.

Key Takeaways

  • Treasury is critical to bank operations
  • Management of capital, liquidity, and funding is essential
  • Interbank lending and LIBOR are key funding mechanisms
  • Regulatory capital requirements constrain operations
  • Stress testing ensures resilience to market shocks
  • Risk management is integral to treasury function
